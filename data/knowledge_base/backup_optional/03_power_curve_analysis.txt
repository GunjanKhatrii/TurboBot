WIND TURBINE POWER CURVE ANALYSIS
==================================

INTRODUCTION
============
The power curve represents the relationship between wind speed and electrical power output. Understanding and analyzing power curves is essential for:
- Performance monitoring and optimization
- Early detection of degradation
- Warranty compliance verification
- Energy production forecasting
- Identifying underperformance causes

THEORETICAL POWER CURVE
========================

Basic Physics:
Power in wind: P = 0.5 × ρ × A × V³
Where:
- P = Power (Watts)
- ρ = Air density (kg/m³)
- A = Swept area (m²)
- V = Wind speed (m/s)

Betz Limit:
Maximum theoretical efficiency: 59.3% (Betz coefficient = 0.593)
Practical turbines achieve: 35-45% in optimal conditions

Typical 2MW Turbine Power Curve:
Wind Speed (m/s)    Expected Power (kW)    Notes
3 (cut-in)          50-100                 Startup phase
4                   150-250                Ramping up
5                   300-400                
6                   500-650                
7                   700-900                
8                   950-1,200              
9                   1,250-1,500            Approaching rated
10                  1,550-1,750            
11                  1,750-1,900            
12 (rated)          1,950-2,000            Rated power
13-25               2,000                  Constant at rated
25 (cut-out)        0                      Safety shutdown

POWER CURVE REGIONS
===================

Region 1: Below Cut-in (0-3 m/s)
- Turbine stationary or idling
- No power production
- Waiting for sufficient wind
- Minimal mechanical stress

Region 2: Partial Load (3-12 m/s)
- Variable speed operation
- Power increases with cube of wind speed
- Maximum efficiency operation
- Most common operating range
- Blade pitch optimized for power capture

Region 3: Full Load (12-25 m/s)
- Constant power output at rated capacity
- Blade pitch actively controlled
- Generator at maximum capacity
- Structural loads managed
- Most revenue-generating region

Region 4: Above Cut-out (>25 m/s)
- Emergency shutdown for safety
- Blades feathered (turned edge-to-wind)
- Mechanical brakes applied
- Turbine secured
- Potential for extreme loads

MEASURING POWER CURVE PERFORMANCE
==================================

Standard Measurement (IEC 61400-12-1):
- Minimum 180 hours of valid data
- 10-minute average values
- Wind speed measured at hub height
- Corrections for air density
- Multiple wind speed bins (0.5 m/s intervals)
- Statistical analysis of each bin

Data Requirements:
- Simultaneous wind speed and power measurements
- Anemometer calibration records
- Air density measurements (temperature, pressure, humidity)
- Turbine availability records
- Exclusion of abnormal operations

Bin Method:
1. Divide wind speeds into 0.5 m/s bins (3.0-3.5, 3.5-4.0, etc.)
2. Calculate average power for each bin
3. Require minimum data points per bin (30+ recommended)
4. Plot measured vs. expected power
5. Calculate performance ratio for each bin

PERFORMANCE METRICS
===================

Capacity Factor:
CF = (Actual Energy / Rated Power × Time) × 100%
- Typical: 25-35% onshore, 40-50% offshore
- Indicates site wind resource utilization

Availability:
Availability = (Operational Hours / Total Hours) × 100%
- Target: >95% for modern turbines
- Excludes scheduled maintenance

Performance Ratio:
PR = Measured Power / Expected Power
- Ideal: 1.0 (100%)
- Acceptable: >0.95 (95%)
- Warning: <0.90 (90%)
- Critical: <0.85 (85%)

Annual Energy Production (AEP):
AEP = ∑(Power at wind speed × Frequency of wind speed)
- Predicted using site wind distribution
- Compared to actual production
- Warranty guarantees often 95-98% of predicted

UNDERPERFORMANCE CAUSES
========================

1. BLADE ISSUES
---------------

Blade Erosion (Leading Edge):
Impact on Performance: 5-15% power loss
Detection Methods:
- Visual inspection (pitting, roughness)
- Performance monitoring (curve deviation)
- Acoustic analysis
- Thermal imaging

Symptoms:
- Gradual power curve degradation
- Worse in Region 2 (partial load)
- More pronounced in high turbulence
- Seasonal variation (worse after winter/insects)

Solutions:
- Leading edge protection tape
- Blade coating renewal
- Regular cleaning
- Aerodynamic repairs
Cost: $5,000-$15,000 per blade

Blade Soiling (Dirt, Ice, Insects):
Impact: 2-10% power loss
Symptoms:
- Temporary performance drop
- Recovers after cleaning or weather change
- More common in specific seasons
Solutions:
- Regular cleaning schedule
- Hydrophobic coatings
- Ice detection and heating systems
Cost: $1,000-$3,000 per cleaning

Blade Damage (Cracks, Delamination):
Impact: Variable, can be severe
Detection:
- Annual inspections
- Acoustic emission testing
- Vibration analysis
Solutions:
- Structural repairs
- Blade replacement if severe
Cost: $10,000-$50,000 depending on severity

2. PITCH SYSTEM PROBLEMS
------------------------

Pitch Misalignment:
Impact: 5-15% power loss
Symptoms:
- Consistent offset in power curve
- All wind speeds affected similarly
- May vary by blade (imbalance)

Detection:
- Compare blade pitch angles
- Power curve analysis
- Load measurements (asymmetry)

Causes:
- Calibration drift
- Mechanical wear in pitch bearings
- Encoder errors
- Software issues

Solutions:
- Pitch calibration procedure
- Replace pitch bearings if worn
- Update pitch control software
Cost: $2,000-$8,000 for calibration
      $15,000-$40,000 for bearing replacement

Pitch Control Sluggishness:
Impact: 3-8% power loss
Symptoms:
- Slow response to wind changes
- Overshooting rated power
- Higher loads in gusts

Solutions:
- Check hydraulic/electric pitch system
- Replace batteries (electric pitch)
- Service hydraulic components
Cost: $3,000-$10,000

3. YAW MISALIGNMENT
-------------------

Static Yaw Error (Offset):
Impact: Cosine losses - cos²(angle)
- 5° error: ~1% loss
- 10° error: ~3% loss  
- 15° error: ~7% loss
- 20° error: ~12% loss

Causes:
- Anemometer mounting error
- Wind vane misalignment
- Nacelle twist
- Yaw bearing issues

Detection:
- Compare nacelle position to wind direction
- SCADA trend analysis
- LIDAR measurements
- Power curve by direction sector

Solutions:
- Yaw calibration (offset correction in software)
- Anemometer realignment
- Yaw bearing inspection
Cost: $1,500-$5,000 for calibration
      $20,000-$60,000 for bearing replacement

Dynamic Yaw Error:
Impact: 2-5% power loss
Symptoms:
- Turbine doesn't track wind changes quickly
- Frequent yaw movements
- High yaw activity in variable winds

Solutions:
- Adjust yaw control parameters
- Service yaw brake system
- Check yaw motor condition
Cost: $2,000-$8,000

4. GENERATOR AND CONVERTER ISSUES
----------------------------------

Generator Inefficiency:
Impact: 2-5% power loss
Causes:
- Winding degradation
- Cooling issues
- Bearing friction
- Magnetic field weaknesses

Detection:
- Electrical efficiency testing
- Temperature monitoring
- Vibration analysis
- Power quality measurements

Solutions:
- Generator servicing
- Cooling system maintenance
- Bearing replacement
- Rewinding (if severe)
Cost: $5,000-$20,000 for service
      $50,000-$150,000 for replacement

Converter Losses:
Impact: 1-3% power loss
Normal converter efficiency: 96-98%
Degraded efficiency: <95%

Causes:
- Component aging
- Inadequate cooling
- Harmonic issues

Solutions:
- Converter servicing
- Cooling fan replacement
- Filter replacement
Cost: $3,000-$15,000

5. CONTROL SYSTEM ISSUES
------------------------

Suboptimal Control Settings:
Impact: 3-10% power loss
Causes:
- Conservative setpoints
- Outdated firmware
- Site-specific optimization not done
- Noise or load reduction active

Solutions:
- Control parameter optimization
- Firmware updates
- Performance-based control tuning
Cost: $2,000-$8,000 for optimization

Wrong Air Density Correction:
Impact: 2-5% power measurement error
Cause: Incorrect temperature/pressure/humidity inputs
Solution: Verify and correct sensor readings
Cost: Minimal, mostly software

6. MECHANICAL LOSSES
--------------------

Gearbox Inefficiency:
Normal efficiency: 96-98%
Degraded efficiency: <95%

Impact: 2-4% power loss
Causes:
- Oil degradation
- Bearing wear
- Gear wear
- Lubrication issues

Detection:
- Oil analysis
- Vibration analysis
- Temperature monitoring
- Torque/power measurements

Solutions:
- Oil change
- Bearing replacement
- Gear inspection/replacement
Cost: See gearbox maintenance document

Brake Drag:
Impact: 1-3% power loss
Symptoms:
- Consistent power loss across all wind speeds
- Brake pad heating
- Abnormal noise

Causes:
- Hydraulic pressure not releasing
- Mechanical binding
- Control system fault

Solutions:
- Brake system inspection
- Hydraulic system service
- Replace brake pads if worn
Cost: $2,000-$6,000

ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS
=====================

Air Density Effects:
Standard conditions: 15°C, 1013 mbar, sea level
Density ratio: ρ/ρ₀

Power adjustment: P_actual = P_standard × (ρ/ρ₀)

Temperature Effects:
- Cold air: +1% power per 10°C decrease
- Hot air: -1% power per 10°C increase
- Range: ±5% typically

Altitude Effects:
- Sea level: ρ = 1.225 kg/m³
- 1000m: ρ = 1.112 kg/m³ (-9%)
- 2000m: ρ = 1.007 kg/m³ (-18%)

Turbulence Intensity:
- Low TI (<10%): Optimal performance
- Medium TI (10-15%): Slight reduction
- High TI (>15%): 2-5% power loss

Wake Effects:
- Directly behind turbine: 60-80% power loss
- 3D downstream: 40-50% power loss
- 5D downstream: 20-30% power loss
- 10D downstream: 5-10% power loss
- 20D+ downstream: Minimal effect

POWER CURVE VERIFICATION PROCEDURE
===================================

Step 1: Data Collection (3-6 months)
- Continuous SCADA data recording
- 10-minute averages
- Meteorological data
- Turbine availability status
- Filter out:
  * Curtailment periods
  * Grid outages
  * Maintenance periods
  * Sensor errors

Step 2: Data Quality Control
- Check for sensor drift
- Remove outliers (>3 standard deviations)
- Verify anemometer calibration
- Check data completeness (>90% per bin)

Step 3: Binning and Analysis
- Create 0.5 m/s wind speed bins
- Calculate average power per bin
- Apply air density corrections
- Plot measured vs. expected curve

Step 4: Performance Assessment
For each wind speed bin:
- Calculate performance ratio
- Identify bins with PR < 0.95
- Investigate root causes
- Prioritize by impact (energy loss)

Step 5: Root Cause Analysis
For underperforming bins:
- Check blade condition
- Verify pitch angles
- Assess yaw alignment
- Review control settings
- Inspect mechanical components

Step 6: Corrective Actions
Prioritize by ROI:
ROI = Annual Energy Gain / Cost of Correction

Example priorities:
1. Low-cost, high-impact (control tuning, yaw alignment)
2. Medium-cost, medium-impact (blade cleaning, pitch calibration)
3. High-cost, high-impact (blade repair, generator service)
4. High-cost, low-impact (defer until scheduled maintenance)

WARRANTY CONSIDERATIONS
=======================

Typical Warranty Requirements:
- Power curve guarantee: 95-98% of warranted curve
- Measurement period: 6-12 months
- Verification: Independent third party
- Corrections: Air density, availability, curtailment

Underperformance Remedies:
1. Verification testing (manufacturer's expense)
2. Corrective actions within 90 days
3. Power curve buy-down (financial compensation)
4. Component replacement (if defective)
5. Turbine replacement (extreme cases only)

Documentation Requirements:
- Detailed SCADA data
- Maintenance records
- Grid availability records
- Meteorological data
- Curtailment logs
